The 30-second version

This goes at the top of the README and anywhere you describe the project. Written em-dash-free, per your repo style rule. Four angles on the same plan.

★ Recommended
A

Method-first (the anchoring story)

"Models anchor on their first idea and spend the rest of the answer defending it. /be-smarter makes any model go broad before it commits: question the framing, generate real options, weigh the tradeoffs, then converge. It calibrates how much thinking each task deserves, runs headless by default, and has a dial that puts you in the loop when you want to be one of the thinkers."

Why this angle: names a failure every agent user has watched happen, then presents the protocol as the fix. Works standalone for the 32-tool audience that has never heard of decision-kit.

B

Origin-first (the decision-kit story)

"Decision-kit made models smarter by accident: even when humans rubber-stamped every recommendation, forcing divergent-then-convergent thinking improved the output. /be-smarter extracts that engine and ships it alone. No picking a skill, no sitting through pages. Just better thinking on any task, in any of the 32 tools that read SKILL.md, with a human-in-the-loop dial when the stakes earn it."

Why this angle: your own founding insight, told as a discovery. Great for people who know decision-kit; weaker as a cold open for strangers.

C

Family-first (the two-sentence split)

"Decision-kit puts you in the loop. /be-smarter thinks that way even when you're not: the same divergent-then-convergent method, calibrated to the stakes of each task, leaving a machine-readable trace you can audit any time. Step in with one flag when a call is yours to make."

Why this angle: crispest line of the four and positions both projects at once. Risk: leads with a product the new audience doesn't know.

D

Outcome-first (the promise)

"One skill that makes your coding agent think before it codes: frame the problem, weigh real alternatives, commit with reasons, and leave a trace. Works in 32 tools. Headless by default, human-in-the-loop when you want it. Trivial tasks stay fast; hard ones finally get the deliberation they deserve."

Why this angle: punchy and benefit-dense, reads like a tagline. Slightly oversells "coding agent" when the protocol is task-general.
