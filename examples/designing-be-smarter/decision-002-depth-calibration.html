Decision 2 of 6 · The Throttle

How does the skill decide how much thinking a task deserves?

Your own complaint about decision-kit — "it's time consuming" — is the adoption killer this decision exists to fix. Run the full double diamond on "rename this variable" and /be-smarter becomes /be-slower. The research says miscalibrated depth isn't just slow, it's wrong: easy tasks degrade under deliberation.

Builds on Decision 1 (double diamond): the protocol has natural collapse points — full two diamonds, solution-diamond only, question-scaffold only, or just answer. Calibration picks among them.
Research Context
  • "Easy inputs rarely require expensive reasoning and can even degrade under excessive deliberation; genuinely difficult inputs benefit disproportionately." The marginal value of thinking is highly query-dependent — this is the consensus finding of the adaptive test-time-compute literature. Reasoning on a Budget survey (arXiv 2507.02076), CODA (2603.08659)
  • Models both overthink AND underthink, measurably. OptimalThinkingBench exists precisely because models can't be trusted to self-pace without structure: thinking models overthink simple queries; non-thinking models underthink hard ones. arXiv 2508.13141
  • Difficulty can be assessed cheaply, without training. Training-free difficulty proxies derived from the generation process itself effectively allocate budget across queries — a cheap upfront triage is a validated pattern, not overhead. OpenReview ztGHhyicWs, BEST-Route (2506.22716)
  • Stakes and reversibility are the practitioner's triage axes. The "one-way vs two-way door" heuristic (Bezos) is the de-facto standard for how much deliberation a decision deserves: irreversible + high-stakes earns the full treatment; reversible + cheap should be decided fast and corrected later.
  • Decision-kit already validated user-facing depth control. /overdecide and /underdecide exist because one size didn't fit — but they put the burden on the user to know the right depth before thinking starts, which is exactly what's hard to know upfront.
The gear range (shared by all options below)
G0Just answer — protocol skipped, one line states why (trivial, factual, reversible)
G1Question scaffold — expert questions + assumptions ledger, single converge
G2Solution diamond only — framing accepted, decisions + options + tradeoffs run
G3Full double diamond — framing challenged, then solution space
G4Double diamond + adversarial escalation on the hardest calls
Options
A

Model self-selects, no ceremony

A one-paragraph instruction: "match depth to the task; here's the gear range." The model picks a gear silently and runs. No rubric, no logged score.

Zero overhead; reads naturally in a skill file

Frontier models are decent at coarse difficulty judgment

OptimalThinkingBench's whole finding: unstructured self-pacing miscalibrates both directions

Unauditable — when output is shallow you can't see why it chose G1

B

User flags only

Depth is the human's job: /be-smarter --quick|--deep (mirroring /underdecide and /overdecide). Default is G3. No model judgment involved.

Fully predictable; user always knows what they bought

Familiar pattern from decision-kit's modifier orchestrators

Requires knowing the right depth before the thinking that would reveal it

Default-G3 on everything re-creates the "time consuming" complaint verbatim

★ Recommended
C

Scored triage + mid-task escalation

A fixed-cost Phase 0: score the task on stakes × ambiguity × reversibility × novelty (1–3 each), map the total to a gear, and log the one-line scoring so it's auditable. Escalate a gear mid-task if tripwires fire (a load-bearing assumption found false, options surprisingly close, success criteria unclear). User flags override everything.

0Score: stakes 2 · ambiguity 3 · reversibility 1 · novelty 2 = 8 → G3
⚡Tripwire: "assumption X is false" → escalate G2→G3, log it
⚑--quick / --deep / --gear=N override the score

Cheap-triage-then-allocate is the validated pattern from the routing literature

Auditable: the score line explains every depth choice after the fact

Escalation catches the tasks that look easy until you start

The rubric is a new thing to design well — bad weights = systematic miscalibration

Slight fixed cost on every invocation (a few sentences)

D

Always full protocol

No calibration. You invoked /be-smarter; you get the double diamond. Trivial tasks shouldn't be invoking the skill at all.

Simplest possible spec; nothing to tune

Invocation itself is arguably the depth signal

Dies the moment the skill is set always-on (CLAUDE.md) — which is exactly the "make any model smarter by default" ambition

Easy tasks measurably degrade under deliberation — this option ignores the central finding

How to respond: Reply with Option A, B, C, or D — or add because… / but…, ask for more options, or describe your own approach.

Recommendation: C. It's the only option that fixes the "time consuming" complaint AND the underthinking risk, with a paper trail. B's flags fold in as overrides, so the user keeps total control without carrying the calibration burden. The rubric design risk is real but bounded — it ships in the spec where contributors can tune it.
